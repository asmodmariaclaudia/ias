What is IoT?

The Internet of Things (IoT) refers to a network of physical devices—such as appliances, vehicles, sensors, and other objects—embedded with software, sensors, and connectivity that allow them to collect, exchange, and act on data over the internet. These devices can operate autonomously or be controlled remotely, enabling smarter decision-making, automation, and enhanced convenience in areas like healthcare, manufacturing, transportation, and home living. As IoT continues to grow, it is transforming everyday objects into intelligent, interconnected systems.

The Internet of Things (IoT) is revolutionizing the way we live and work by connecting billions of devices—from smart thermostats and wearable fitness trackers to industrial sensors and autonomous vehicles. These devices gather and share data to enable automation, efficiency, and real-time insights. However, this rapid expansion comes with significant security concerns. Many IoT devices are developed with limited computing resources and minimal built-in security, often prioritizing functionality and cost-efficiency over protection. As a result, they become vulnerable points of entry for cybercriminals.

Introduction to IoT Threats

IoT threats refer to the various cybersecurity risks associated with the use and integration of connected devices in networks. These threats include unauthorized access, data breaches, device hijacking, denial-of-service (DoS) attacks, and the exploitation of software vulnerabilities. The diversity of devices, lack of standardized security protocols, and often-infrequent firmware updates make it difficult to manage and secure the growing IoT ecosystem effectively. Personal Privacy Invasion

Smart home devices can be hacked, exposing private data or enabling live surveillance without consent.

Business Disruption

Compromised IoT systems can halt operations, manipulate data, and damage reputations.

Data Breaches

Unsecured IoT devices can leak personal or sensitive info, leading to identity theft or fraud.

Botnets & DDoS Attacks

Hijacked devices may be used in large-scale cyberattacks, like the 2016 Mirai botnet incident.

Public Safety Risks

Attacks on healthcare or traffic systems can put lives at risk by disrupting critical services.

Regulatory Consequences

Non-compliance with data laws can result in legal penalties and loss of public trust.
